Unmask

Designing a calmer dating experience for neurodivergent adults to enhance user engagement and promote meaningful connections.

UX Design / Graphic Design

UX Designer / UX Researcher / UI Designer

Overview

Unmask is a conceptual dating app designed to reduce the social and sensory barriers often experienced by neurodivergent adults.

Over two semesters, I researched user needs, designed interactive prototypes, conducted usability testing, and iterated on the experience based on real user feedback.

The result is an accessibility-focused app centered on meaningful communication rather than endless swiping.

User Personas

To better understand the target audience, I created two personas, Alex and Jordan, representing neurodivergent adults navigating dating. They highlighted key needs, goals, and frustrations, like sensory sensitivities and a desire for meaningful connections, and guided design decisions to make Unmask inclusive and user-centered.

User Journey Maps

User journey maps highlighted key pain points and motivations, revealing opportunities to create smoother, sensory-friendly interactions. These insights guided interface decisions and feature design to support meaningful connections.

Research Insights & Direction

The research phase established a clear foundation for the Unmask experience. Through a competitive audit of existing dating platforms and analysis of user feedback, key gaps emerged around accessibility, emotional safety, and authentic connection, particularly for neurodivergent users.

User personas and journey maps helped translate these insights into concrete design priorities, revealing moments of cognitive overload, social pressure, and friction within typical dating flows. These findings directly informed the decision to design Unmask as a sensory-friendly dating app focused on clarity, intentional pacing, and meaningful connections rather than surface-level interactions.

By grounding design decisions in research, Unmask evolved from a general dating concept into a purpose-driven product tailored to the needs of neurodivergent users.

Logo and Brand Design

For the Unmask brand, I began by prompting AI tools to explore initial logo concepts centered around the idea of masking and identity. I then iterated the design through three refinements, improving clarity, accessibility, and visual balance at each stage.

The final logo was designed to align with the app's values, while the color palette was carefully selected to minimize sensory overload and create a welcoming, friendly experience for neurodivergent users.

Paper Wireframes

The paper wireframes served as a critical foundation for Unmask's design decisions. I began by sketching five variations of each core page, allowing me to quickly explore different layouts, information hierarchies, and interaction flows without the constraints of digital tools.

From these initial concepts, I created refined wireframes as needed, narrowing in on solutions that supported clarity, reduced cognitive load, and aligned with the app's accessibility goals before moving into high-fidelity design.

Low-Fidelity Figma Prototype

After creating an initial low-fidelity prototype in Figma, I gathered peer feedback to identify usability issues and opportunities for improvement. This feedback helped refine layout clarity, reduce cognitive load, and improve the overall flow of key interactions.

Before Feedback

  • Navigation felt unclear
  • User profile needed detailed preferences
  • Design lacked visual hierarchy

After Feedback

  • Added Nav bar for clearer navigation
  • User profile details added
  • Improved visual design

Testing & Iteration

While the first semester focused on building Unmask, the second semester centered on validating the experience through user testing and continuous refinement.

Using moderated interviews, unmoderated usability studies, preference testing, and additional survey feedback, I evaluated how users interacted with the prototype and identified opportunities to improve clarity, navigation, and overall usability.

Rather than treating the first high-fidelity prototype as the finished product, I approached it as the beginning of an iterative design process, making informed improvements based on real user feedback instead of assumptions.

Validation Through User Testing

Throughout the second semester, I shifted my focus from creating new features to evaluating how well the existing experience supported users.

To validate design decisions, I conducted several forms of user research:

  • Moderated usability interviews
  • Unmoderated usability testing
  • Preference testing
  • Follow-up user surveys

Rather than asking whether users liked the design, these studies focused on identifying usability issues, reducing cognitive load, and validating accessibility decisions.

Design Improvements

Stronger Navigation

Persistent bottom navigation and clearer page hierarchy helped users move through the app more confidently.

Reduced Cognitive Load

Layouts were simplified, unnecessary interface elements were removed, and visual hierarchy was refined to create a calmer experience.

Enhanced User Profiles

Profile pages evolved to better communicate personality, communication preferences, and compatibility without overwhelming users.

Reflections

This project fundamentally changed how I approach UX design.

When I began, I assumed good design came from creativity.

Over time I learned that good design comes from listening.

Several of my favorite ideas were removed after testing, while seemingly small changes, such as simplifying navigation or restructuring onboarding, had a much larger impact on the user experience than expected.

The biggest takeaway was understanding that research is not simply the first phase of UX. It is the foundation that guides every design decision throughout the entire product lifecycle.

If I continued this project, I would focus on conducting additional moderated usability studies with neurodivergent participants and expanding the accessibility features based on their feedback.
